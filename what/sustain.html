Laboratory Wave Tank Tests

The University of Miami Rosenstiel School of Marine and Atmospheric Science houses the world’s largest indoor hurricane simulator and wave tank, the Surge Structure Atmosphere Interaction facility (SUSTAIN).

Credit: Julie Ruiz-Merchan and Joel Amendolara

Our team of researchers uses this unique facility to examine the wave effects of model hybrid reef breakwaters made of coral skeletons and simple construction materials. These models are scaled down to fit inside the 75 ft long x 20 ft wide x 6.5 ft tall wave flume and are placed on the “seafloor” in front of an artificial beach within the tank.

Credit: Julie Ruiz-Merchan and Joel Amendolara

Waves, programmed to reflect conditions that we see both on a day-to-day basis and during tropical storm conditions off our shorelines in Miami Beach, are generated to pass over the hybrid reef breakwater model. We carefully measure wave height and energy at points along a cross section of the tank: before, on top of, and after the barrier.

Credit: Julie Ruiz-Merchan and Joel Amendolara


What do we discover?

Shorebound wave energy is reduced by our hybrid reef breakwater in two ways. The majority of wave energy is dissipated as the wave “feels” the change in depth due to the solid structure (the gray infrastructure breakwater model) placed on the “seafloor”. Additionally, wave energy can be further dissipated by the friction that is created by a structurally complex coral model. This component is often referred to as the friction component; in the natural world where denser reefs with more living coral (i.e., less eroded reefs) are present, the friction component is higher.

Credit: Julie Ruiz-Merchan and Joel Amendolara

Measurements of wave height along the cross section of our model hybrid reef breakwater are analyzed to better understand potential dynamics in our real-world shorelines: under what conditions do hybrid reef breakwaters work best and what are the relative contributions to wave height reduction by the breakwater and coral fragments individually.
