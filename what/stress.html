Use of Climate-Resilient Corals

It’s not enough to simply restore corals back to an environment in which they are still threatened. In addition to many local threats, climate-driven ocean warming causes a process called thermal bleaching. In this process, corals lose the single-celled symbiotic algae (called zooxanthellae) as a stress response to higher water temperatures. This process can be fatal for the coral, because they rely heavily on their symbionts for energy and survival.

To ensure that the corals outplanted on our hybrid reef breakwaters in the waters off Miami Beach are themselves resilient to climate change, we will outplant corals to the reef breakwaters on Miami Beach that are sourced from sites in southern Biscayne Bay (~15-25 km to the south) where we have identified a population of more thermally tolerant corals.

Further, we will test whether stress-hardening approaches (i.e., preemptively exposing corals to stress to better prepare them for future stress) can be used to change the algal symbionts of outplanted corals in favor of thermotolerant types, and also assess the effects of both of these approaches in increasing coral survivorship.

Stress-hardened corals will be deployed side-by-side with control corals that have not been stress-hardened, and heat-tolerant genotypes from southern Biscayne Bay will be deployed next to corals collected from local sites on Miami Beach.

This will enable us to assess the effects of both of these approaches in increasing coral survivorship. These approaches will help increase the climate resilience of our outplants and reduce mortality in the event of climate-induced episodes of coral bleaching.
