Role of Natural & Nature-based Barriers

Gray infrastructure is a broad term that is used to describe man-made structures, typically made of concrete, metal, quarried rock, or a combination of materials. In the context of coastal protection, gray infrastructure are built structures that help offset or reduce coastal pressures like waves and flooding. Seawalls, rip-rap, and breakwaters are examples of gray infrastructure.

Green and gray infrastructure

Green (or blue) infrastructure is the strategic use of natural habitats to meet the same needs as those met by gray infrastructure. In the context of coastal protection, green infrastructure includes such natural elements as marshes, mangroves, and coral reefs used in coastal restoration efforts to help stabilize and defend the shoreline.

Both gray and green infrastructure have advantages and disadvantages. Gray infrastructure can be costly and break down over time if not well-maintained, thus requiring regular maintenance and repair over time. It is also aesthetically displeasing and potentially detrimental to natural habitats if not well-planned. At the same time, gray infrastructure has the advantage of being immediately effective in protecting the coastline. In contrast, green infrastructure may take a long time to get to the stage where it becomes effective for coastal protection.

Coastal habitat restoration generally starts with small seedlings and fragments of plants and coral respectively, which can take years to grow to the height or density of a breakwater or seawall. What makes green infrastructure attractive however is that it is part of the natural environment, self-growing and therefore self-sustaining for the most part and, beyond coastal protection, it also provides ecosystem benefits by creating habitat services such as nursery and spawning grounds for organisms.

To create an effective coastal protection project, it is important to take all these factors into account. In some cases, gray and green infrastructure can be merged to form hybrid or nature-based infrastructure that combine their advantages and overcome to some degree their disadvantages. For the ULINK project, we merge active coral restoration with submerged breakwaters to make an efficient hybrid reef breakwater that achieves multiple goals: reduction of wave height, replenishment of endangered species, and restoration of critical habitat.
