Value of Coral Reefs

Why are our coral reefs important?

Coral reefs are some of the most valuable ecosystems on the planet because they provide a number of benefits called ecosystem services. Over a half billion people globally rely on these services for food, income, and protection. These services include:

  • Marine biodiversity: Coral reefs provide refuge, food, and spawning and nursery habitat for more than one million marine species, including more than 4,000 fish species. In fact, despite occupying less than one percent of marine habitat, reefs support the life of about 25% of all marine organisms.

  • Fisheries and food: Both small-scale artisanal and large-scale industrial fisheries rely on coral reefs for catches of fish, lobster, and other marine organisms eaten around the world. In the U.S., these fisheries are valued at roughly $100 million per year.

  • Tourism and Recreation: Coral reefs provide income to millions of people around the world, contributing nearly 9.6 billion dollars to the tourism and recreation industry, by attracting diving, snorkeling, and recreational fishing activities.

  • Coastal Protection: Coral reefs act as the first line of defense against ocean waves, flooding, and storms, and reduce wave energy by up to 97%. This translates into protection for both valuable coastal infrastructure and human lives.

  • Medicines: Certain chemicals from corals and other invertebrates have been found to be effective in fighting bacterial infections.

Globally, these services equate to as much as $172 billion per year.
